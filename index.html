1️⃣Meeting 1: Meet & Greet

  • 15–20 min phone call (block 30 min to be safe)
  • Go through their balance sheet — assets, debts, income, expenses
  • Understand their goals: retirement age, income needs, legacy wishes
  • Identify what they want to focus on most
  • Input their data into eMoney financial planning software
  • Key outcome: Book Meeting 2 on the call

2️⃣Meeting 2: eMoney Financial Plan

  • Show their current plan with NO changes made
  • Walk through projections, cash flow, balance sheet in eMoney
  • Reveal gaps — "If you do nothing, here's where you land"
  • Sometimes skipped — go straight from Meeting 1 to Meeting 3
  • Key outcome: Create urgency around the need to act

3️⃣Meeting 3: Morningstar Investment Review

  • Input all current holdings into Morningstar
  • Run a full Portfolio X-Ray analysis
  • Review: past performance, risk, fees, concentration, benchmark
  • Compare to S&P 500 — highlight best/worst years
  • Show concentration risk and underperformance clearly
  • Key outcome: Prospect sees the problem clearly

4️⃣Meeting 4: Investment Recommendations

  • Present the Action Plan: Short / Intermediate / Long-term buckets
  • Active management option: 1% fee, actively traded managed accounts
  • Registered Index Linked Annuity options (0% fee, downside protection)
  • Often a combination of both strategies
  • Compare current portfolio to proposal side by side
  • Key outcome: Present the ask — can get a yes right here

5️⃣Meeting 5: Decision & Onboarding

  • Yes or no
  • If yes: begin paperwork and account transfer process
  • Sometimes the decision comes at Meeting 4 — be ready
  • If no: understand the objection and schedule a follow-up

🎯The 6 Planning Pillars

  • 🏖️ Retirement Planning — income projections, Social Security optimization
  • 📈 Investment Strategies — active management + protected growth options
  • 💰 Tax Strategies — Roth conversions, tax-loss harvesting
  • 🏛️ Estate & Legacy Planning — trusts, beneficiaries, wealth transfer
  • 🛡️ Protection Planning — life insurance, long-term care
  • 💼 Wealth Management — holistic coordination of everything

Brendan's Scripts — Appointment Setting
Brendan — your ONLY job is to get them to agree to a meeting, qualify them, find their pain, and then book a time on AJ's calendar. Keep it warm, simple, and confident.
Brendan's Golden Rule: You are NOT selling financial planning. You are scheduling a qualified conversation. If someone asks a financial question — anything about investments, fees, specific strategies — your answer is always: "That's a great question — and that's exactly what AJ will be able to walk you through on the call. I'm just here to get you scheduled, but he'll answer all of that for you."
Never mention specific fees, specific investment products, or investment strategies. AJ handles all of that in the meeting. Your job is the calendar.

Role Plays & Objection Handling
Every objection is a question in disguise. Practice these until your responses feel natural — not scripted. The prospect's words are shown first, then multiple ways to handle it.

RP-01 · "I'm Not Interested"

Easy▼
"I'm not interested."
Rebuttal Options:
I completely understand — and I'm not calling to sell you anything today. I'm just looking to see if a complimentary financial review would make sense for your situation. Can I ask — do you already have a solid plan in place for [retirement income / your finances going forward]?
Fair enough — and most people say that before they hear what it actually involves. This isn't a pitch. It's a complimentary analysis of your own finances, at no cost to you — no products, no pressure. The only question is whether it'd be worth 15 minutes of your time. Would that be a flat no, or more of a "not right now"?
[Brendan] Totally fair. If I could just ask — is it the timing, or is it more that you're not really looking to do anything with your finances right now? I want to make sure I'm not wasting your time either.
They're not saying no to the offer — they're protecting their time. Separate "not interested in this call" from "not interested in their own financial wellbeing." Redirect to their situation, not the service.

RP-02 · "I Already Have a Financial Advisor"

Medium▼
"I already have a financial advisor."
Rebuttal Options:
[AJ] That's great — and I'm not here to replace anyone. What I do is a completely independent second opinion on your current portfolio and financial plan. At no cost to you. In my experience, even people who've had an advisor for years often find things that have been overlooked — fees they weren't aware of, concentration risks, gaps in their retirement projections. Would a second set of eyes on your situation be something you'd be open to?
[AJ] Totally understand. Out of curiosity — when's the last time your advisor ran a full portfolio analysis on your accounts and compared your performance to the S&P 500 benchmark? A lot of advisors are great at relationship management but don't necessarily do that level of analysis. I'd love to show you what that looks like, at no cost, with no strings attached.
[Brendan] Oh, that's great! AJ actually works alongside people who already have advisors quite often — it's more of a second-opinion type thing. He's not looking to replace anyone, just give you an independent outside look. Would that be worth a quick call?
If They Say They're Happy and Not Open to Change:
[Both] That's completely fair — and the last thing I want to do is step on anyone's toes. If anything ever changes or you'd want a second opinion down the road, I'd love to be the person you call. I'll make a note and maybe follow up with you in a few months. Thanks for your time!
Don't compete with their current advisor. Position yourself as additive, not replacing. If they're happy and closed, back out gracefully — protecting the relationship is worth more than a forced meeting.

Rebuttal Options:

"Send me something" is almost always a soft brush-off, not a genuine request. If you send without booking a call, follow-through drops to nearly zero. Get the call first — confirm with email as a secondary step.

Don't challenge their competence — that will shut them down immediately. Position the value as additional institutional tools and information, not a replacement for their judgment. Their confidence is actually an asset — engaged investors are often the best prospects.

Part of what I help people figure out is finding the right balance between protection and growth — and there are options specifically designed for people who want their money working for them without the full volatility exposure. [AJ] Market anxiety is completely legitimate — and it's one of the most common things I work through with people. The goal isn't to convince you to do anything specific. Validate the fear — never dismiss it. Then introduce the inflation cost of inaction, which is equally real. You're not telling them to dive into the market — you're showing them that "doing nothing" has its own risks and that having a clear plan beats reacting.

🎯Your ONE Job

You are not a financial advisor. You are not a salesperson. You are a scheduler. Your entire job on every call is to get one outcome: qualify the prospect and book a meeting for AJ. That's it.   • Smile when you talk. People can hear a smile — it changes your tone.
  • Stand up when you call if possible. Your voice projects better standing.
  • Don't apologize for calling. You're offering something valuable at no cost to them.
  • Pause after asking a question. Let the silence work for you — don't fill it.

❓When They Ask Finance Questions

If someone asks anything you don't know — investment types, tax strategies, specific numbers — do NOT try to answer. Say:

"That's a great question — and that's exactly what AJ would be able to walk you through on the call. I'm just here to get you scheduled, but he'll be able to answer all of that."

This positions AJ as the expert and creates a clear reason for the meeting. Use it every time.

🚫Things To NEVER Say or Do

  • Never say "it's free" — say "complimentary" or "at no cost to you"
  • Never say "I'm sorry to bother you"
  • Never say "whenever you're free" — always offer specific time options
  • Never discuss specific fees or investment products
  • Never try to explain financial strategies in detail
  • Never keep talking after they say yes — book it and close
  • Never argue if they're rude — gracefully exit and move on
  • Never end a call without a next step

🧠The Psychology of a Cold Call

  • The first 10 seconds decide if they keep listening — be warm and clear immediately
  • Stating the specific reason for your call (their job status change) is far more effective than being vague
  • The word "quick" lowers resistance — always say "quick 15 minutes"
  • Asking permission ("I'll keep this super quick, okay?") gets a micro-yes
  • People don't say no to you — they say no to uncertainty. Reduce uncertainty.
  • Silence after a question is your friend — don't fill it, let them answer
  • Your goal on the qualification questions is to make them feel heard, not interrogated

📋The Qualification Questions — Why They Matter

  • They make AJ's first call much more focused and valuable
  • They help you uncover the pain point AJ should lead with
  • They filter out people who aren't actually a fit (e.g., happy with current advisor and not open to change)
  • They make the prospect feel heard before the meeting even happens
  • The 6-pillar question is where you find the real reason they'll say yes

🔄Handling Rejection — The Right Mindset

  • Every "no" is just practice for the next "yes"
  • A rejection is not personal — they're rejecting the timing or the situation, not you
  • Expect most calls to not book — that's completely normal
  • After a tough call: take 10 seconds, reset, then dial again. Don't carry it.
  • Keep a tally. Progress is motivating — track your calls and your bookings.

Quick Vocabulary Guide
You'll hear these terms — here's what they mean in plain English. You don't need to explain these — just know enough not to be caught off guard.
Financial Plan

A full picture of someone's money situation — what they have, what they owe, what they earn, and how they reach their goals. AJ builds this using professional software.

eMoney

Software AJ uses that shows someone's full financial picture and models out what happens if they do nothing vs. if they make changes. Like a GPS for money.

Morningstar

A professional tool that analyzes investments. Shows what someone owns, how it's performed, what risk they're taking, and how fees compare. Like an MRI for a portfolio.

Portfolio X-Ray

A Morningstar report that breaks down all of someone's accounts in one place — shows the full picture across everything they own.

401(k) / Retirement Account

A savings account through an employer where money grows for retirement. This is usually what they have at their old job that needs to be dealt with after a career change.

Pre-tax vs. Roth

Pre-tax means they get a tax break now but pay taxes later. Roth means they paid taxes already so withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. If they don't know which they have, AJ will figure it out.

Balance Sheet

A list of everything someone owns (house, savings, investments) vs. everything they owe (mortgage, debt). Like a snapshot of their full financial picture.

Holistic Planning

Looking at ALL of someone's finances together — investments, taxes, insurance, estate plans, retirement income — rather than just one piece of it.

⚖️ KEY TERM: Fiduciary

AJ is a fiduciary — which means he is legally and ethically required to act in the client's best interest at all times, not the interest of any brokerage, fund company, or insurance carrier.

Why this matters on a call: Most people have never worked with a fiduciary. Many brokers operate under a lower "suitability" standard — they just need to sell you something "suitable," not necessarily what's best for you. A fiduciary is held to a higher legal standard. If a prospect asks why they should meet with AJ vs. their current advisor, this is often the most powerful answer.

Quick phrase [Brendan]: "One thing that separates AJ is that he's a fiduciary — he's legally required to put your interests first, not a firm's."

The 6 Pillars — Quick Reference
🏖️ Retirement Planning

When can they retire, how much income will they have, how long will it last, Social Security timing and strategy.

📈 Investment Strategies

Active management with ongoing rebalancing and a passive protected-growth option. Organized into short, intermediate, and long-term buckets.

💰 Tax Strategies

Roth conversions, tax-loss harvesting, retirement account sequencing, minimizing taxable events year over year.

🏛️ Estate & Legacy

Beneficiary review, trust planning, wealth transfer to heirs, charitable giving strategies — making sure assets go exactly where intended.

🛡️ Protection Planning

Life insurance, long-term care coverage, disability insurance — making sure the financial plan is protected if something unexpected happens.

💼 Wealth Management

Coordinating all of the above into one cohesive plan. One advisor, full picture — nothing falling through the cracks.

Top 5 Objections — Quick Answers
# Objection Quick Response
1 "I have an advisor" This is an independent second opinion, not a replacement. Are you open to investment recommendations?
2 "Not interested" Is it the timing, or do you already have a solid plan in place? The review is at no cost — would it hurt to just see where you stand?
3 "Send me something" Absolutely — let me ask one quick question so what I send is actually relevant. And let's also pencil in a call so it's useful.
4 "What's the catch?" No catch. The planning process is at no cost to you. The only way I make money is if you choose to work with me after seeing everything.
5 "Talk to my spouse" Have them both on the call — it's actually better that way. Can we find a time where you're both available?
